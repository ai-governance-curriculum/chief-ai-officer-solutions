# Module 105 — Quiz Answer Key

Answers and explanations for the [Module 105 quiz](https://github.com/ai-governance-curriculum/chief-ai-officer-learning/blob/main/lessons/mod-105-responsible-ai-and-ethics/quiz.md).

---

## Section A — What ethics is and is not (§1)

**Q1. c. Values and reasoning.** Per §1.1, ethics's
source of authority is the most fragile of the four
disciplines and rests on values + reasoning rather than
external law or quantitative models.

**Q2. False.** §1.3 is explicit: a principles document
is not an ethics program.

**Q3.** Any answer noting that ethics theater is a
sub-pattern of governance theater (mod-101 §6), or that
both describe a program producing visible apparatus
without changing behaviour. Example: "ethics theater is
governance theater wearing ethics vocabulary — the
program produces ethics statements, training modules,
and dashboards without changing what decisions get made."

**Q4. d.** Producing universally-agreed answers is **not**
characteristic of working CAO ethics work; per §1.3, many
ethics questions do not have consensus answers and
forcing one is itself unethical.

---

## Section B — The principles landscape (§2)

**Q5. c. OECD AI Principles.** Per §2.1.

**Q6. True.** §2.2's convergence problem.

**Q7. c.** Identical across CAOs in the same industry is
**not** required — §2.4 notes the framing set should be
*locatable in at least one authoritative document* (so
not idiosyncratic), but it does not need to match other
CAOs' choices.

---

## Section C — Bias and fairness (§3)

**Q8. b.** Predictive parity, equal false-positive
rates, and equal false-negative rates. This is the
Chouldechova / Kleinberg result.

**Q9. False.** §3.2 is explicit: a model that satisfies
all common metrics simultaneously is either operating in
a base-rate-equal context, using a metric set that hides
the trade-off, or wrong.

**Q10.** Any answer naming the under-specification of
subgroup discovery — no single tool reliably finds all
relevant subgroups; the discipline is running several
methods, treating their union as the analysis space, and
documenting methods.

**Q11. b.** Predictive parity + equal opportunity for
the qualified population is suggested in §3.3 as the
defensible credit-decisioning metric set.

---

## Section D — Transparency (§4)

**Q12. d. The CAO's children.** The §4.1 taxonomy names
eight audiences: regulator, internal validator, senior
management/board, external business partners (deployers),
affected parties, affected communities, internal
developers, curious external public.

**Q13. d. All of the above.** §4.4 names all three
failure modes of mechanical explainability.

**Q14.** Any answer naming a category from §4.5 — the
most direct is *process transparency*. Other defensible
answers include data-side transparency (datasheets),
system-context transparency, or workflow transparency.

---

## Section E — Contestability and recourse (§5)

**Q15. d. Statistically-significant volume of past
contestations** is **not** required. §5.1 lists named
decision, named decider, path, timeline, resource, and
outcome.

**Q16. b. Contestation timelines that exceed the harm
window** is a §5.4 anti-pattern. (a), (c), and (d) are
features of working processes.

**Q17.** Any answer distinguishing them per §5.2.
Example: "contestability is the affected party's ability
to challenge the system's decision through a formal
channel; recourse is what the affected party can do
*outside* contestation to address their concern."

---

## Section F — Operationalizing ethics (§6)

**Q18. b. Naming a disagreement honestly.** §6.5 names
this as the most underused ethics tool.

**Q19. d. Universal stakeholder consensus on every
position.** §6.6 names the three things — (a), (b),
(c) — and explicitly does *not* require consensus.

**Q20.** Any answer from §6.4. Example: "Help: when the
ethics committee has defined scope and is consulted on
cases that fit that scope. Don't: when the committee is
consulted on everything, becoming a bottleneck — or on
nothing, becoming ceremonial."
