# Exercise 05 — Capstone: Year-3 Program Synthesis — Reference Solution

## 1. Solution overview

End-of-year-3 synthesis for **Halverston
Capital**. The program is materially mature
(NIST Level 3 across all four functions); the
substantive issues are no longer foundation
gaps but second-order questions about how the
program scales and how the CAO operates at
year-3 level. Four substantive errors named
honestly. Forward posture: operate the program
at scale; contribute to industry conversation;
build the function's continuous-succession
readiness.

This is the synthesis at the closing of the
foundational track. It is not a board report;
it is the document written to a successor.

## 2. The synthesis

> **HALVERSTON CAPITAL — AI PROGRAM YEAR-3
> SYNTHESIS**
> *Author: Chief AI Officer. For: AI Risk
> Council, Audit Committee, eventual successor.*

### Where the program is

**Maturity:** NIST Level 3 (Defined) across
GOVERN, MAP, MEASURE, MANAGE. The third-party
governance gap from years 1-2 is closed; vendor
AI risk overlay operating. The cross-LOB taxonomy
uniformity work from year 2 is complete; all
three LOBs operate against the same taxonomy
with the same interpretations.

**Operating effectiveness:** Two material
incidents in the trailing 24 months
(vendor LLM swap year 2; small-scale bias
incident year 3 — pattern detected at the leading
indicator before threshold crossing). Both
handled credibly; post-incident reviews
substantive; recommendations tracked through
closure.

**Risk posture:** Within appetite across all
categories. Risk register has 23 material risks;
all have treatment plans; all residuals are
named and accepted at the appropriate level.

**Compliance state:** ISO 42001 certification
achieved year-3 Q2. EU AI Act high-risk system
documentation current. State insurance regulators
across Halverston's footprint: no open material
matters.

**Talent state:** 12 net new roles since program
start; team mature; AI Risk Lead operating at
deputy CAO level; deputy hiring completed in
year 3. Key-person dependency on the CAO
materially reduced.

### What changed since year 2

Year 3's substantive changes:

- **Third-party governance overlay operational.**
  The long-deferred gap, closed. Vendor AI
  risk assessment is now in production for all
  AI vendor onboarding and renewal.
- **Cross-LOB taxonomy uniformity achieved.**
  The year-2 underperformance, closed. Public
  markets, private credit, and wealth advisory
  operate against the same vocabulary.
- **Tier 2 monitoring automation deployed.** All
  Tier 2 systems now have automated
  monitoring; no more manual operating cadence.
- **Per-system onboarding time reduced from 8
  weeks to 5 weeks.** Operating-efficiency goal
  partially achieved; 4-week target deferred to
  year 4 as the marginal cost rises.
- **Deputy CAO recruited.** Material change in
  the CAO function structure.
- **ISO 42001 certification.** Material
  external validation.
- **One external industry contribution.** The
  CAO published an opinion piece on the EU AI
  Act's post-market monitoring requirements in
  a peer-reviewed risk publication.

### What works that is durable

Five things from the past three years that have
proven durable and should be preserved:

1. **The split-authorship boundary pattern.**
   Used with MRM (mod-104), CISO (mod-107),
   and Compliance Officer (mod-109). All three
   boundaries operate cleanly. The pattern
   should be preserved through any operating
   model changes.
2. **The single-named-lead pattern for
   incidents.** Operating since year 1. Has
   handled two material incidents and multiple
   smaller events without coordination
   failures. Should be preserved.
3. **The pre-computed notification matrix.**
   Updated annually since year 1. Has met every
   notification window since the program began.
   Should be preserved.
4. **The audit ledger evidence layer.**
   Operating per mod-108 since year 1. ISO
   42001 audit specifically called out the
   evidence quality. Should be preserved.
5. **The three-cadence review discipline.**
   Per mod-109 §3 — operating, steward, audit
   cadences. Has caught issues at the
   operating cadence before they materialised.
   Should be preserved.

### What's fragile

Four substantial fragilities:

1. **Vendor concentration on identity /
   attestation infrastructure.** Despite
   multi-vendor strategy intent (mod-106 Ex-05),
   the secondary vendor has not been brought to
   active duty. The primary vendor's outage or
   pivot would be substantively disruptive. Year
   4 priority: validate the secondary vendor
   pathway under simulated migration conditions.
2. **The AI Risk Lead's accumulated knowledge.**
   Despite the deputy hire, much of the program's
   subtle operational knowledge lives in the AI
   Risk Lead's head. Documentation closes some
   of this, but not all. If the AI Risk Lead
   leaves before further knowledge transfer,
   the deputy alone would not fully cover.
3. **Wealth advisory AI usage growth outpacing
   monitoring.** Wealth advisory has grown its
   AI usage substantively in year 3; the
   monitoring infrastructure is current but
   has not scaled in advance of the growth.
   Year 4: monitoring scale-up.
4. **Regulator relationships dependent on the
   current CAO personally.** Years 1-3 of
   relationship-building have created
   substantial credit; the credit is
   substantially with the CAO personally rather
   than with the function. Year 4: deliberate
   handoff of one regulator relationship to
   the deputy.

### What we got wrong

The hardest section. Four substantive things the
CAO and the program have done over three years
that, in hindsight, would have been done
differently:

1. **Under-invested in vendor AI risk capability
   in year 1.** The third-party governance gap
   was deferrable but not as long as we
   deferred it. By the time we addressed it in
   year 3, we'd accumulated 2.5 years of
   vendor decisions that should have had
   AI-specific assessment. In hindsight, even a
   partial overlay in year 1 would have been
   right.

2. **Over-invested in tabletops in year 1.**
   Three tabletops in year 1 was too many. Two
   were confirmatory rather than substantive.
   The performance-art trap in mod-110 §6.6,
   recognised honestly. Year 2 corrected; the
   year-1 cost was 4-6 weeks of team time we
   couldn't recover.

3. **Deferred cross-LOB taxonomy uniformity to
   year 2.** It should have been year 1 work.
   The year-2 underperformance was the
   consequence; we lost a year's worth of
   cross-LOB integration. In hindsight, even
   compressing year-1 to make room would have
   been right.

4. **Hired the Algorithm Validation Lead 6
   months later than the function needed.**
   The role was identified in the year-1 plan
   but we held the position for budget reasons
   into year 2 mid-year. The cost: 6 months of
   MRM-relationship lag and one specific
   instance where a borderline model deployment
   would have benefited from earlier
   validation-lead engagement.

These are not the only errors, but they are the
substantive ones. The pattern across them: each
was a defensible call at the time and proved
wrong in hindsight. The discipline going forward
is not to avoid all such errors (impossible) but
to recognise them faster.

### Year-4 and beyond

The program does not need a year-4 plan
articulated here; the AI Risk Council will own
year-4 planning. The posture and direction:

- **Operate at scale.** Per-system onboarding
  to 4 weeks; portfolio doubling sustainable
  without function doubling.
- **Mature regulator engagement.** Deeper
  relationships; more proactive on
  regulatory-evolution conversations.
- **Industry voice.** The CAO and the program
  contribute publicly to the AI governance
  conversation. Not for vanity; for the
  organisation's reputation and the program's
  recruiting.
- **Strategic contribution.** The CAO is in
  the room when Halverston is making AI-
  strategic decisions, not just being asked
  whether the decision is acceptable.
- **Continuous succession readiness.** The
  function operates at a level where any
  qualified successor could take it. The
  current CAO's discipline is making the
  function not depend on the current CAO.

### Personal reflection

(Optional, included.)

Three years in role. The work has required
discipline I didn't have at the start: the
discipline of being substantive in conversations
where being substantive is uncomfortable;
the discipline of being honest in self-
assessment when honest is unwelcome; the
discipline of being replaceable when being
indispensable is the more flattering posture.

The work has given the chance to build
something that didn't exist. The function
operates today, three years in, in a way that
the organisation now depends on. That's worth
having done.

I don't know where I go from here. I know what
I won't do: I won't stay in the role until I'm
the limiting factor. The successor will come;
when they do, the function will be ready.

### What the board should know

Three things not otherwise in the report:

1. **The CAO's succession is on track.** The
   deputy is at year-2-CAO level after 12
   months. By year 5, the deputy or a peer
   would be a credible CAO successor.
2. **One regulator relationship is at material
   inflection.** The EU AI Act NCA for
   Halverston's Dublin operations has a
   personnel change scheduled Q2 year 4;
   relationship continuity through the change
   will matter. We are aware and preparing.
3. **The program's industry voice is
   beginning.** One year-3 publication;
   inclusion in two industry working groups.
   The board should expect this to grow in
   year 4-5; the CAO will surface specific
   commitments before accepting them.

## 3. Reasoning notes

- **Why the "what we got wrong" section is
  substantive.** §6.3 mod-111 — programs that
  cannot honestly name errors over three years
  are not credible. The four errors are real
  and recognisable to anyone who has built a
  function. Naming them honestly builds the
  document's credibility.
- **Why I included the personal reflection
  section despite being optional.** The
  synthesis is the closing document; the
  personal posture matters. Programs that
  treat the CAO as interchangeable with the
  function get under-developed CAOs over time.
  Reflecting honestly on the role itself is
  the right discipline.
- **Why "succession is on track" is the first
  thing the board should know.** By year 3,
  the board is genuinely asking this. Surfacing
  it directly in the synthesis is honest and
  shows the discipline.
- **Why I didn't write the year-4 plan.** The
  AI Risk Council owns year-4 planning; the
  CAO at year-3 end sets posture and
  direction, not detailed plans. Distinguishing
  these is the year-3 discipline.
- **What this synthesis deliberately doesn't
  do.** Compare Halverston to peer
  institutions in detail; provide specific
  metrics that would belong in a quarterly
  board report; substitute for the AI Risk
  Council strategic review. It is the CAO's
  written-to-successor view; other documents
  serve other purposes.
